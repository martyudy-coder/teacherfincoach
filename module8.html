Your 4 Retirement Income Sources

A comfortable teacher retirement comes from four sources working together:

1. Pension (30-50% of final salary)

Your state pension system provides guaranteed monthly income for life. The exact amount depends on your years of service and final average salary. Most teachers get 40-60% of their working income from pension.

2. Social Security (20-30% of final salary)

Social Security provides additional guaranteed income. The average teacher receives $1,500-$2,500/month. Note: Some states (CA, TX, IL, OH, MA, etc.) have pension systems that reduce or eliminate Social Security‚Äîcheck your state.

3. 403(b) and/or 457(b) (Fills the 30% Gap!)

Your voluntary retirement accounts fill the income gap between your pension/Social Security and your actual retirement needs. This is where YOU control your financial future.

4. HSA (Tax-Free Healthcare Fund)

If you have a high-deductible health plan, your HSA provides tax-free money for healthcare costs in retirement. Healthcare expenses average $165K-$315K per couple even WITH Medicare.

  • Social Security: $24,000/year (if eligible in your state)

  • HSA withdrawals: $8,000/year (tax-free for medical expenses)

Top 10 Teacher Retirement Mistakes (And How to Avoid Them)

Don't make these common (and costly) mistakes:

‚ùå Mistake #1: Cashing Out When Changing Jobs

The disaster: Taking $40,000 as cash when switching districts. After taxes and penalties, you get $26,000. That $40,000 would've been $400,000 at retirement.

The fix: ALWAYS roll over to new employer plan or IRA. Use direct rollover. Never take a check made out to you.

‚ùå Mistake #2: Paying High Fees

The disaster: Staying with a 1.5% fee vendor for 30 years. Cost: $250,000+ in lost wealth.

The fix: Check 403bwise.org. Red or yellow light? Switch to green light vendor. Target fees under 0.20% total.

‚ùå Mistake #3: Not Starting Early Enough

The disaster: Waiting until age 40 to start saving. Missing 15 years of compounding = $300,000+ less at retirement.

The fix: Enroll in your first year of teaching. Even 3-5% is better than zero. Time is your biggest advantage.

‚ùå Mistake #4: Stopping Contributions During Market Crashes

The disaster: Panic selling in 2008 and stopping contributions. Missing the 2009-2020 bull market recovery = $200,000+ lost.

The fix: NEVER stop contributing during market downturns. You're buying shares on sale. Keep contributing or increase if possible.

‚ùå Mistake #5: No Emergency Fund (Then Raiding Retirement)

The disaster: $3,000 car repair, no emergency fund, withdraw from 403(b). Taxes + penalties + lost growth = $40,000+ true cost.

The fix: Build a 6-month emergency fund FIRST. Keep it separate from retirement accounts. Never touch retirement for emergencies.

‚ùå Mistake #6: Ignoring the Employer Match (If Offered)

The disaster: Some districts match contributions up to 3-5%. Not contributing = leaving free money on the table.

The fix: If your district offers a match, contribute AT LEAST enough to get the full match. It's instant 100% return.

‚ùå Mistake #7: Forgetting About HSAs

The disaster: Eligible for HSA but never opening one. Missing triple tax advantage + $200,000+ tax-free healthcare fund.

The fix: If you have a high-deductible health plan, open and max out an HSA. It's the best retirement account that exists.

‚ùå Mistake #8: Wrong Asset Allocation

The disaster: Being 100% bonds at age 30 (too conservative) or 100% stocks at age 64 (too aggressive).

The fix: Use a target-date fund OR follow age-based allocation (stocks = 110 minus your age). Rebalance annually.

‚ùå Mistake #9: No Beneficiaries Listed

The disaster: Dying without beneficiaries on file. Account goes through probate. Family waits months/years to access money.

The fix: Designate primary AND contingent beneficiaries. Update after major life events (marriage, divorce, births, deaths).

‚ùå Mistake #10: Not Increasing Contributions After Raises

The disaster: Getting 3% raises every year but never increasing retirement contributions. Lifestyle inflation eats all raises.

The fix: Every time you get a raise, increase retirement contributions by 1-2%. You won't miss it, and it compounds into six figures.

Glossary: Key Terms

Quick reference for all the terms used in this course:

403(b)
Tax-advantaged retirement account for teachers and nonprofit employees. Contributions are pre-tax (reduce taxable income now), growth is tax-free, withdrawals in retirement are taxed as income. 2026 limit: $24,500 ($32,500 if 50+).
457(b)
Tax-advantaged retirement account for government employees. Similar to 403(b) but with one major advantage: no 10% early withdrawal penalty if you separate from service. 2026 limit: $24,500 ($32,500 if 50+). Can contribute to BOTH 403(b) AND 457(b) in the same year.
Asset Allocation
How your money is divided between stocks, bonds, and cash. Younger investors typically hold 80-90% stocks for growth. Older investors shift toward 60-70% stocks and 30-40% bonds for stability.
Compound Interest
Earning returns on your returns. Your money grows exponentially over time. Example: $10,000 at 8% annual return becomes $100,627 in 30 years without adding a single dollar.
Expense Ratio
Annual fee charged by mutual funds and ETFs, expressed as a percentage. Example: 0.04% fee on $100,000 = $40/year. Keep total fees under 0.20% for maximum wealth building.
HSA (Health Savings Account)
Triple tax-advantaged account for people with high-deductible health plans. Tax-deductible contributions, tax-free growth, tax-free withdrawals for qualified medical expenses. 2026 limits: $4,400 (individual), $8,750 (family).
Index Fund
Mutual fund or ETF that tracks a market index (like S&P 500 or Total Stock Market). Low-cost, diversified, passive investing. Beats 90%+ of actively managed funds over time.
Pension
Guaranteed monthly income for life provided by your state's teacher retirement system. Calculated based on years of service and final average salary (usually highest 3-5 years). Most teachers receive 40-60% of working income from pension.
Rebalancing
Adjusting your portfolio back to target allocation. Example: If stocks grow from 80% to 90%, you sell some stocks and buy bonds to get back to 80/20. Target-date funds rebalance automatically.
Rollover
Moving retirement money from one account to another without taxes or penalties. Example: Rolling 403(b) from old district to new district or to an IRA. Always choose "direct rollover" to avoid withholding.
Roth 403(b)
After-tax version of 403(b). You pay taxes NOW on contributions, but growth and withdrawals in retirement are 100% tax-free. Good for young teachers in low tax brackets who expect higher taxes in retirement.
Target-Date Fund
All-in-one fund that automatically adjusts from aggressive (stocks) to conservative (bonds) as you approach retirement. Pick the fund with the year closest to your retirement (e.g., Target 2055 if retiring in 2055). Simple, effective, automatic.
Vesting
The number of years you must work before you own your pension benefits. Most states require 5-10 years of service. If you leave before vesting, you may lose pension benefits (but you always keep your own 403(b)/457(b) contributions).

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: I'm 45 and haven't started saving. Is it too late?
A: NO! It's not too late. You have 20 years until retirement. If you save aggressively (15-20%) and use catch-up contributions after age 50, you can still build $300K-$500K. That's $1,200-$2,000/month in retirement income on top of pension. Start TODAY.
Q: Should I pay off my mortgage or save for retirement?
A: Do BOTH. Contribute at least 10-15% to retirement while making regular mortgage payments. Don't sacrifice retirement to pay off a low-interest mortgage faster. The tax-deferred growth in your 403(b) typically beats the mortgage interest rate.
Q: My district only has high-fee vendors. What should I do?
A: First, ask HR to add Fidelity or Vanguard to the approved list (many districts will). Second, pick the BEST available option even if not perfect (Module 3 fee tiers). Third, contribute anyway‚Äîa high-fee 403(b) is still better than no 403(b). You can roll over to a low-fee IRA when you leave the district.
Q: Should I contribute to 403(b) or 457(b) first?
A: If you can only max one, choose 457(b) because of the no-penalty early withdrawal advantage. If you can max both, do it! Combined limit: $49,000/year (or $65,000 if 50+). Most teachers start with 403(b), add 457(b) later as income grows.
Q: What if the market crashes right before I retire?
A: This is why you shift to bonds as you age (60% stocks / 40% bonds by age 60). The bond portion provides stability during crashes. You can delay retirement by 1-2 years if needed to let the market recover. Or use the 4% rule‚Äîwithdraw from bonds while letting stocks recover.
Q: Can I retire early as a teacher?
A: Yes, but it requires planning. Many pension systems allow retirement with reduced benefits after 20-25 years of service. You'll need a larger 403(b)/457(b) balance to cover the gap until full pension and Social Security kick in. Healthcare before Medicare (age 65) is the biggest challenge.
Q: How much should I have saved by age 40? Age 50?
A: Age 30: 1x annual salary. Age 40: 3x annual salary. Age 50: 6x annual salary. Age 60: 8-10x annual salary. These are ballpark targets‚Äîif you're behind, don't panic. Increase contributions and use catch-up after 50.
Q: Should I do traditional 403(b) or Roth 403(b)?
A: For most teachers, traditional (pre-tax) is better because you're in a higher tax bracket now than you'll be in retirement. Roth makes sense if: (1) you're early in your career with low salary, (2) you expect to be in a higher tax bracket in retirement, or (3) you want tax diversification. When in doubt, choose traditional.
Q: My vendor charges 1.2% fees. Should I switch?
A: YES. Absolutely. A 1.2% fee will cost you $100,000-$200,000+ over a career compared to a 0.04% fee. Check 403bwise.org for your vendor's rating. If red or yellow, switch to a green-light vendor (Module 7 covers exactly how to do this).
Q: What's the 4% rule?
A: Withdraw 4% of your retirement account balance in year 1 of retirement, then adjust for inflation each year. Example: $500,000 balance = $20,000/year in withdrawals. Historically, this makes your money last 30+ years without running out.
Q: Should I invest in individual stocks?
A: NO. Stick with low-cost index funds. Individual stock picking is gambling, not investing. 90%+ of professional fund managers can't beat the S&P 500 over time. You won't either. Buy the whole market through index funds and let it compound.
Q: How do I know if I'm on track?
A: Use the Income Gap Calculator to see if your current savings rate will close the 30% income gap. Check your balance against the age milestones (1x salary by 30, 3x by 40, 6x by 50). If you're behind, increase contributions now.
Q: What happens to my 403(b) when I die?
A: It goes to your named beneficiaries immediately (bypasses probate). This is why designating beneficiaries is CRITICAL. If no beneficiaries are listed, it goes through your estate (probate = months or years of delays). Update beneficiaries after every major life event.
Q: Can I contribute to an IRA AND a 403(b)?
A: Yes! 403(b) limit ($24,500) and IRA limit ($7,000) are separate. You can contribute to both. Max your 403(b) first, then add IRA contributions if you have extra money. Roth IRA is especially good for younger teachers.
Q: I got laid off. What do I do with my 403(b)?
A: You have 4 options: (1) Leave it where it is, (2) Roll to new employer plan, (3) Roll to IRA, (4) Cash out (NEVER do this). Best option: Roll to an IRA at Fidelity or Vanguard for maximum investment control and low fees. Use DIRECT rollover.
