How to Invest: Picking Your Funds

What to do once you've found a green light vendor

You Found a Green Light Vendor. Now What?

In Module 3, you learned about fees and discovered 403bwise. You looked up your district, found the green light vendors, and now you're ready to open an account.

But here's where most teachers get stuck: "What do I actually invest in?"

You log into Vanguard or Fidelity, and suddenly you're staring at 50+ investment options with names like:

  • "Vanguard Total Stock Market Index Fund"
  • "Fidelity 500 Index Fund"
  • "Target Date 2055 Fund"
  • "Bond Index Fund"

Don't panic. This module will teach you exactly what these are, how to choose, and how to build a simple portfolio that will serve you well for decades.

Coach Marty says:
Most teachers overthink this step. They want to pick the "perfect" investments and end up paralyzed by choice. Here's the truth: simple beats complicated every time. The difference between a "good" and "great" portfolio is tiny compared to the difference between high fees and low fees. You've already won the big battle by choosing a green light vendor. Now let's keep it simple.

Why Index Funds?

The funds offered in most 403(b) plans are index funds ‚Äî and that's a good thing.

What's an index fund? It owns a little piece of EVERYTHING in the market. If there are 500 companies in the S&P 500, an S&P 500 index fund owns all 500 of them.

Why they're great:

  • Ultra-low fees (0.03% - 0.15%)
  • No one is trying to "beat the market" ‚Äî you just GET the market's return
  • Historically, the stock market averages about 10% per year over long periods
  • They outperform 95% of "actively managed" funds over 15+ years
Quick note: If you ever open a Roth IRA or brokerage account outside of work, you may encounter salespeople pushing "actively managed" funds with higher fees. Stick with index funds there too. The same principles apply.

Stocks vs. Bonds: Getting the Mix Right

Within index funds, there are two main categories:

Stock Funds (Equities)

  • Ownership in companies
  • Higher returns over time (average 10% per year)
  • More volatile (can drop 30-50% in bad years)
  • Best for long-term growth

Bond Funds (Fixed Income)

  • Loans to governments or companies
  • Lower returns (average 4-5% per year)
  • Less volatile (typically drop 5-15% in bad years)
  • Provide stability and reduce swings

How to Allocate Based on Your Age

The general principle: younger investors can handle more risk (stocks), older investors need more stability (bonds).

Here's a practical guide:

Your Age Stock % Bond % Strategy
25-35 90-100% 0-10% Aggressive growth - you have time to recover from downturns
35-45 80-90% 10-20% Still growth-focused with slight stability cushion
45-55 70-80% 20-30% Balanced approach as retirement nears
55-65 60-70% 30-40% More stability, less volatility
65+ 40-60% 40-60% Preserve wealth while still growing
Coach Marty says:
If you're 35 and panicking about a 30% stock market drop, remember: you have 27 years until retirement. That "crash" is actually a buying opportunity‚Äîyour contributions are buying stocks at a discount. Young teachers should embrace volatility. It's how wealth is built.

The Easy Button: Target Date Funds

If you want the simplest possible approach, choose a Target Date Fund.

How they work:

  • Teachers typically select the fund with a date closest to when they'll retire
  • The fund automatically adjusts from stocks to bonds as you age
  • Young? It's mostly stocks. Getting closer to retirement? It shifts to bonds.
  • You never have to rebalance or make changes

Example Target Date Funds

‚úÖ Is a Target Date Fund Right for You?

Many teachers choose a Target Date Fund if they:

  • Want the simplest approach possible
  • Don't want to think about rebalancing
  • Are okay with "good enough" instead of optimizing every detail
  • Want to set it and forget it

At Vanguard: Look for "Target Retirement 20XX" funds (fees around 0.08%)

At Fidelity: Look for "Fidelity Freedom Index 20XX" funds (fees around 0.12%)

The DIY Approach: The 3-Fund Portfolio

If you want slightly more control and even lower fees, you can build your own portfolio with just THREE funds.

This is called the "3-Fund Portfolio" and it's recommended by investing legends like Jack Bogle (founder of Vanguard).

The Three Funds

1
U.S. Total Stock Market Fund
Examples: Vanguard Total Stock Market Index (VTSAX), Fidelity Total Market Index (FSKAX)
What it does: Owns ~4,000 U.S. companies
Typical allocation: 60-70% of portfolio
2
International Stock Market Fund
Examples: Vanguard Total International (VTIAX), Fidelity International Index (FTIHX)
What it does: Owns companies in Europe, Asia, emerging markets
Typical allocation: 10-30% of portfolio
3
Total Bond Market Fund
Examples: Vanguard Total Bond Market (VBTLX), Fidelity U.S. Bond Index (FXNAX)
What it does: Owns U.S. government and corporate bonds
Typical allocation: Based on your age (see table above)

Coach Marty says:
The 3-fund portfolio has fees around 0.03-0.05%, while target date funds are around 0.08-0.12%. Over 30 years, that difference is maybe $10,000-15,000. So yes, the 3-fund portfolio is cheaper, but target date funds are simpler. Pick based on whether you want to save an extra 0.05% or have complete autopilot. Both are excellent choices.

Making Your Decision

Here's a simple decision tree to help you choose:

üéØ Common Decision Paths

1
Do you want absolute simplicity?
If yes: Many teachers select a Target Date Fund with a year closest to their retirement (age 65-67).
If no: Continue to Step 2.
2
Are you willing to rebalance once per year?
If yes: A popular approach is building a 3-fund portfolio using the allocation table based on your age.
If no: Consider going back to Target Date Fund (Step 1).
3
Log into your 403(b) account
Once logged in, look for "Change Investments" or "Investment Options." Find your chosen fund(s) by name. Set your contribution to 100% to your chosen fund(s).
4
Set up your contributions
Go to your HR/payroll system and set up automatic contributions. Start with whatever you can afford‚Äîeven $100/month makes a difference. You can always increase later.

Wondering how contributions affect your take-home pay? Try our Paycheck Impact Calculator to see exactly how much your paycheck changes after taxes.
Important: Don't let perfection be the enemy of good. Choosing a Target Date Fund and starting NOW is infinitely better than spending 6 months researching the "perfect" portfolio and never starting. You can always change your investments later.

Red Flags: What NOT to Choose

Avoid these even at green light vendors:

  • Anything with "Managed" in the name - These are actively managed funds with higher fees
  • Sector-specific funds - "Technology Fund," "Healthcare Fund," "Real Estate Fund" are too narrow and risky
  • Individual stocks - You're not a stock picker. Don't try to be one.
  • Funds with expense ratios over 0.25% - If you're at a green light vendor, you shouldn't see fees this high
  • Anything with "Growth" or "Value" in isolation - Total market funds own both, which is what you want
Coach Marty says:
Every year, some tech stock or cryptocurrency will skyrocket 500%. Teachers see this and think "I should have invested in that!" But here's the reality: for every winner, there are 10 losers. Betting your retirement on individual stocks is like betting your retirement on lottery tickets. Boring index funds win over decades. Always.

Real Teacher Examples

üìñ Rachel's Simple Approach

Rachel is 32, teaches elementary school, and doesn't want to think about investing. She chose Vanguard Target Retirement 2055 Fund and set up automatic contributions of $400/month.

That's it. She doesn't check her balance monthly. She doesn't worry about the market. She knows the fund automatically shifts from stocks to bonds as she ages.

By retirement, she'll likely have $800,000+ without ever making another investment decision.

üìñ David's 3-Fund Portfolio

David is 45 and likes having more control. He set up a 3-fund portfolio at Fidelity:

  • 50% Fidelity Total Market Index (U.S. stocks)
  • 20% Fidelity International Index (International stocks)
  • 30% Fidelity U.S. Bond Index (Bonds)

Once per year, he checks if any fund has drifted more than 5% from target. If so, he rebalances. Takes him about 15 minutes annually.

His total fees: 0.04% per year. Over 30 years, he'll save about $20,000 compared to a target date fund‚Äîbut he has to do that annual rebalancing.

Coach Marty says:
Both Rachel and David made excellent choices. Rachel values simplicity. David likes saving that extra 0.05% in fees. There's no "wrong" answer here. The only wrong choice is staying with a high-fee vendor or never starting at all. Pick what works for you and move forward.

üìå Key Takeaways

  • Choose index funds over actively managed funds‚Äîlower fees, better long-term performance
  • Target Date Funds are the easy button‚Äîpick the year closest to retirement and you're done
  • The 3-Fund Portfolio (U.S. stocks, international stocks, bonds) offers slightly lower fees with more control
  • Your age determines your stock/bond mix‚Äîyounger means more stocks, older means more bonds
  • Avoid sector funds, individual stocks, and anything with fees over 0.25%
  • Starting NOW beats spending months finding the "perfect" portfolio
