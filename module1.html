The Promise vs. The Reality

When you started teaching, you probably heard some version of this: "Don't worry about retirement‚Äîyou have a pension!"

And that's true! California teachers are part of CalSTRS (California State Teachers' Retirement System), one of the largest and most well-funded pension systems in the United States. It's a defined-benefit pension, meaning you'll receive a guaranteed monthly paycheck for the rest of your life after you retire.

But here's the part they conveniently leave out: Your CalSTRS pension will only replace about 60-70% of your final salary.

Think about that for a second. If you're making $100,000 in your final year of teaching, CalSTRS will pay you about $60,000-$70,000 per year in retirement. That's a $30,000-$40,000 annual income drop.

The 30% Income Gap (This Is the Big One)

Here's the Math:
Your Final Salary: $100,000/year
CalSTRS Pension (70%): $70,000/year
THE GAP: $30,000/year üò±

Over 25 years of retirement:

That's three-quarters of a MILLION dollars in lost income.

And that's NOT accounting for inflation. In reality, the purchasing power gap grows even larger over time. This is why so many retired teachers struggle financially‚Äînot because they were bad with money, but because nobody explained this gap to them when they were young.

Financial planners say you need 80-90% of your pre-retirement income to maintain your standard of living. CalSTRS gets you to 50-70%. That's a 20-40% gap.

The Inflation Factor (This Sneaks Up on You)

CalSTRS pensions include a 2% annual Cost of Living Adjustment (COLA). Sounds good, right?

Except inflation averages 3-4% per year historically. That means your pension's purchasing power slowly DECLINES over time.

Real-World Example:
Year 1 of retirement: $70,000 pension feels comfortable
Year 10: With 2% COLA, you're at $85,000... but inflation means that buys what $60,000 used to buy
Year 20: You're losing purchasing power EVERY year

This is why you need supplemental savings that can grow with (or faster than) inflation‚ÄîNOT just to fill the initial gap, but to keep pace with rising costs over 25-30 years.

Social Security: The Good News for 2025! üéâ

Here's something HUGE that just changed: For decades, teachers got screwed on Social Security benefits. But as of January 2025, that penalty was eliminated. Let me explain what this means for you.

üìö What is the Windfall Elimination Provision (WEP)?

The WEP was a 1983 law that reduced Social Security benefits for people who also received a pension from work where they didn't pay Social Security taxes‚Äîlike teachers with CalSTRS.

Here's why it existed: Social Security's benefit formula is progressive‚Äîit replaces a higher percentage of income for lower earners. But if you worked 10 years in the private sector (paying into Social Security) and 25 years teaching (paying into CalSTRS instead), Social Security would see only your 10 years of reported earnings and think you were a low-income worker. WEP corrected this by reducing your Social Security benefit.

Before WEP Repeal (Pre-2025):
If you earned Social Security credits from a summer job, part-time work, or career before teaching, your Social Security benefit could be reduced by up to $500-600/month. Ouch.
üéâ The 2025 WEP Repeal: What Changed

In December 2024, Congress passed the Social Security Fairness Act, which repealed WEP (and its cousin, GPO‚ÄîGovernment Pension Offset). Effective January 2025, teachers now receive their FULL Social Security benefits based on their work history‚Äîno more penalties!

After WEP Repeal (2025 and Beyond):
If you worked 10+ years in jobs where you paid Social Security taxes (private sector, non-teaching jobs, summer work, etc.), you now get your FULL Social Security benefit‚Äîtypically an extra $300-800/month in retirement. This is HUGE!
üí∞ Real-World Impact: What This Means for You
Scenario 1: Career Changer
You worked in marketing for 12 years (paying Social Security taxes), then became a teacher at 35. Under the old WEP rules, your Social Security benefit would've been slashed by ~$400/month. Now? You get the full benefit you earned‚Äîabout $1,200/month.
Scenario 2: Summer Jobs & Part-Time Work
You worked retail, restaurants, or other jobs during college and summers, accumulating 10 years of Social Security credits. Old rules: benefit reduced by $300-500/month. New rules: Full benefit of ~$800-1,000/month.
Scenario 3: Lifelong Teacher (No Other Jobs)
You started teaching at 22 and never worked jobs that paid Social Security taxes. WEP repeal doesn't affect you directly‚Äîyou weren't eligible for Social Security benefits anyway. But your CalSTRS pension remains unchanged.

‚ö†Ô∏è Important Note:

To qualify for Social Security benefits, you need at least 40 credits (roughly 10 years of work in jobs where you paid Social Security taxes). If you've only taught and never worked other jobs, you won't receive Social Security‚Äîbut you also weren't affected by WEP. Your CalSTRS pension is your primary retirement income.

Bottom line: If you worked non-teaching jobs before or during your teaching career, the WEP repeal could add $300-800/month to your retirement income. That's $3,600-$9,600 per year‚Äîreal money that helps close the 30% gap! Check your Social Security statement at ssa.gov/myaccount to see your estimated benefits.
