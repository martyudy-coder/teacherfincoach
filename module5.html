Your Savings Game Plan

How much to save and the secret weapon (HSA) most teachers miss

You Know the Problem. Here's the Solution.

In Module 1, you learned about the 30% income gap your pension leaves behind. In Modules 2-4, you learned about 403(b) and 457(b) accounts, how to pick vendors, and how to choose investments.

Now it's time to answer the biggest question: "How much should I actually save?"

This module gives you a clear, actionable savings game plan. Plus, we'll cover the secret weapon that most teachers don't know exists: the Health Savings Account (HSA)‚Äîwhich I consider the holy grail of investment accounts.

Here's the truth: Most teachers either save nothing (thinking their pension is enough) or save randomly without a plan. You need a strategy. This module gives you percentages, priorities, and a clear order of operations so you know EXACTLY what to do each paycheck.

The Savings Target: 15-20% of Your Gross Income

Financial experts generally recommend saving 15-20% of your gross income for retirement. This includes:

  • Your pension contributions (already deducted from your paycheck)
  • Your 403(b) and/or 457(b) contributions
  • Any employer matches or contributions

Example: If you make $70,000/year, you're already contributing about 10% to your pension (varies by state). That means you need another 5-10% going into your 403(b) or 457(b) to hit the 15-20% target.

Can't hit 15-20% right away? Start with whatever you can‚Äîeven 3-5%. The key is to START. Then increase by 1% every year when you get a raise. You won't even notice it.

Adjust by Age

Your savings rate can vary based on when you start:

  • In your 20s: 10-15% total is great (you have time on your side)
  • In your 30s: 15% minimum (compound growth is powerful here)
  • In your 40s: 18-20% (you're catching up)
  • In your 50s+: 20-25% if possible (final push, plus catch-up contributions allowed)

The Order of Operations: Where to Put Your Money First

Not all savings vehicles are created equal. Here's the priority order many teachers follow:

1
Emergency Fund
Save $1,000-2,000 in a regular savings account FIRST. This prevents you from raiding retirement accounts for emergencies. Once you have this, move to Step 2.
2

If you have a high-deductible health plan, max out your HSA first. The triple (or quadruple!) tax advantage makes it unbeatable. See section below for details.

Start with whichever account works best for your situation (Module 2 covers this). Contribute 5-10% of your salary to hit your total savings goal.

Once retirement contributions are flowing, build your emergency fund to 3-6 months of expenses. This gives you real financial security.
5
Max out 403(b) and/or 457(b)
If you have extra income, increase contributions toward the annual limits ($24,500 for 2026, $32,500 if you're 50+).

üèÜ Coach Marty's Holy Grail: The Health Savings Account (HSA)

I view the HSA as the holy grail of investment accounts. It allows you to never pay tax on the money you put in, the growth, or when you take it out‚Äîas long as it's used for qualified health expenses. No other account can match this.

What Is an HSA?

A Health Savings Account (HSA) is a tax-advantaged savings account designed for people with high-deductible health plans (HDHPs). Think of it as a retirement account specifically for healthcare costs‚Äîbut with even better tax benefits than a 403(b) or IRA.

What qualifies as a medical expense? HSAs can be used for a wide range of healthcare costs including doctor visits, prescriptions, dental care, vision care, medical equipment, and more. For a complete list, see IRS Publication 502.

‚ö†Ô∏è CRITICAL REQUIREMENT: You Must Have a High-Deductible Health Plan

You can ONLY contribute to an HSA if you have a qualifying high-deductible health plan (HDHP).

2026 HDHP Requirements:

  • Individual plan: Minimum deductible of $1,650
  • Family plan: Minimum deductible of $3,300

How to check if you're eligible:

  • 1. Log into your district's benefits portal or check your health insurance card
  • 2. Look for terms like "High Deductible Health Plan" or "HDHP" or "HSA-eligible"
  • 3. Check your plan's deductible amount (it should meet the minimums above)
  • 4. Call your HR benefits office and ask: "Is my health plan HSA-eligible?"

If you DON'T have an HDHP, you cannot open or contribute to an HSA. But you can switch during open enrollment if your district offers an HDHP option.

Your District Doesn't Offer an HSA? No Problem.

Here's something most teachers don't realize: You don't need your district to offer an HSA to open one.

As long as you have a qualifying high-deductible health plan (HDHP), you can open an HSA account yourself at any major brokerage firm‚Äîcompletely independent of your school district.

‚úÖ How to Open an HSA on Your Own

Step 1: Confirm you have an HDHP (check your health plan documents or ask HR)

Step 2: Choose a brokerage firm that offers HSAs. Popular options include:

  • Fidelity (Coach Marty's favorite‚Äîno account fees, excellent investment options)
  • Lively (simple interface, good for beginners)
  • HSA Bank (widely used, solid platform)

Step 3: Open your HSA account online (takes about 10-15 minutes)

Step 4: Fund your HSA by:

  • Setting up automatic transfers from your checking account, OR
  • Making manual contributions throughout the year

Step 5: Deduct your contributions on your tax return (Form 8889)

The catch: If your district doesn't offer payroll deduction, you'll contribute with after-tax dollars and claim the deduction when you file taxes. You still get the tax benefit‚Äîit just happens at tax time instead of immediately. And you'll miss the payroll tax savings (Social Security/Medicare taxes), but you still get the triple tax advantage.

Bottom line: Even if your district doesn't offer HSA payroll deductions, you can (and should!) open an HSA yourself if you have an HDHP. The tax benefits are too good to pass up.

The Triple Tax Advantage (Unmatched!)

HSAs have a triple tax advantage that no other account offers:

‚úÖ Three Ways HSAs Beat Everything Else

  • 1. Tax-deductible contributions: Money goes in pre-tax (or you deduct it on your tax return), reducing your taxable income RIGHT NOW
  • 2. Tax-free growth: Investments grow without taxes‚Äîno taxes on dividends, capital gains, or interest
  • 3. Tax-free withdrawals: When you use the money for qualified medical expenses, you pay ZERO taxes. Ever.

No other account can say this. 403(b)s are taxed when you withdraw. Roth IRAs don't give you a tax deduction up front. HSAs do BOTH.

The QUADRUPLE Tax Advantage (If Funded Through Payroll)

Here's where it gets even better: If your district allows you to contribute to your HSA through payroll deduction, you get a 4th tax advantage that most people miss.

The 4th advantage: Skip payroll taxes!

When you contribute through payroll deduction (before your paycheck is calculated), you avoid paying:

  • Social Security tax (6.2%)
  • Medicare tax (1.45%)

That's an extra 7.65% savings on every dollar you contribute!

Example: If you contribute $4,400 through payroll deduction, you save an additional $337 in payroll taxes (7.65% √ó $4,400). That's free money stacking up on top of your income tax savings!

What if my district doesn't offer payroll deduction? You can still open an HSA on your own (Fidelity, Lively, HSA Bank). You'll contribute with after-tax dollars and deduct it on your tax return‚Äîyou still get the income tax deduction and the triple tax advantage. You just miss the payroll tax savings.

The Secret Strategy: Invest It Like a 401(k)

Here's what most people miss: You don't have to spend HSA money immediately.

Many HSA providers (Fidelity, Lively, HSA Bank) allow you to invest your HSA balance in stocks, bonds, and index funds‚Äîjust like a 403(b). This is the superpower of the HSA.

The high-level strategy: Pay current medical bills out-of-pocket if you can afford it. Let your HSA balance grow INVESTED for decades. Then in retirement (when healthcare costs skyrocket), you have a TAX-FREE pile of money to cover Medicare premiums, prescriptions, long-term care, etc.

Here's how it works:

  • You contribute to your HSA (pre-tax or tax-deductible)
  • You invest the money in low-cost index funds (same as your 403(b))
  • It grows tax-free for 20-30 years
  • When you need to pay a medical bill, you simply sell some investments and use your HSA debit card to pay‚Äî100% tax-free

You can also choose NOT to invest: If you prefer, you can keep your HSA balance in cash (earning a small amount of interest) and use it for immediate medical expenses. But this means missing out on decades of tax-free growth‚Äîthe real power of the HSA.

Why This Matters for Teachers: Healthcare Costs in Retirement

According to Fidelity's 2024 Retiree Health Care Cost Estimate, an average retired couple age 65 will need approximately $315,000 to cover healthcare and medical expenses throughout retirement. A single person will need about $165,000.

And that's WITH Medicare!

Medicare doesn't cover everything:

  • Medicare premiums (Part B, Part D, Medigap)
  • Prescription drugs (co-pays and deductibles)
  • Dental and vision care (not covered by Medicare)
  • Long-term care (nursing homes, in-home care)
  • Hearing aids

An HSA helps you cover these costs TAX-FREE. That's a massive advantage over pulling money from a taxable 403(b).

What If I Retire Before 65?

Many teachers retire at 55-60. If you retire before Medicare kicks in at 65, you'll need to pay for health insurance yourself. This can cost $10,000-$20,000 per year.

Your HSA can bridge this gap. You can use HSA funds to pay for:

  • Premiums for COBRA continuation coverage
  • Premiums for marketplace health insurance (if you're receiving unemployment compensation)
  • Medicare premiums (Parts A, B, C, D)
  • Out-of-pocket medical expenses

Age 65+: HSAs Become Even More Flexible

Here's a bonus: At age 65 or older, you can withdraw HSA funds for ANY reason‚Äînot just medical expenses.

If you withdraw for non-medical expenses, you'll pay ordinary income tax (just like a traditional IRA), but there's no 10% penalty. This makes the HSA function like a traditional IRA with bonus tax-free medical withdrawals.

üí° PRO TIP: The Receipt Strategy

Keep all your medical receipts! You can reimburse yourself from your HSA YEARS later.

Here's how it works:

  • Pay medical expenses out-of-pocket today (if you can afford it)
  • Keep the receipts/documentation
  • Let your HSA investments grow tax-free for 10, 20, even 30 years
  • Reimburse yourself ANYTIME in the future‚Äî100% tax-free

Example: You pay $5,000 out-of-pocket for medical expenses in 2026. You keep the receipts. In 2036, after that money has grown to $12,000 in your HSA, you can withdraw the original $5,000 tax-free (even though it's been 10 years). Or wait even longer and let it keep growing!

This is the ultimate strategy: Build a massive tax-free medical fund while having access to cash if needed via reimbursement.

The Tax Deduction You Can't Ignore

Every dollar you contribute to an HSA lowers your taxable income.

Example:

  • Your salary: $70,000
  • HSA contribution: $4,400 (individual max)
  • New taxable income: $65,600

At a 22% tax bracket, that's $968 in tax savings just from the contribution. Plus, all the growth is tax-free. Plus, withdrawals for medical are tax-free. Unbeatable.

Even if your district doesn't offer an HSA: As long as you have a qualifying high-deductible health plan, you can open an HSA yourself at Fidelity, Lively, or HSA Bank. You contribute with after-tax dollars from your paycheck, then deduct it on your tax return. The tax benefit is the same.

Real Teacher Examples

üìñ Jessica's 10-Year HSA Strategy

Jessica is 35, makes $65,000/year, and switched to her district's high-deductible health plan with an HSA. Her district contributes $1,000/year, and she contributes $3,400 more ($4,400 total individual max).

She pays current medical bills out-of-pocket (about $1,500/year) and invests 100% of her HSA balance in a total stock market index fund.

After 30 years at 8% average returns:

  • Total invested: $132,000 ($4,400/year √ó 30 years)
  • HSA balance at age 65: ~$498,000
  • Tax-free withdrawals for healthcare in retirement: Every penny

Compare this to paying those medical expenses from a 403(b), where she'd pay income tax on every withdrawal. The HSA saves her $100,000+ in taxes over retirement.

üìñ Marcus and Lisa's Combined Strategy

Marcus (42) and Lisa (40) are both teachers with a family HDHP. They max out their family HSA at $8,750/year.

They use HSA funds for current expenses like braces for their kids ($6,000), vision care ($500), and prescriptions ($1,200/year). The rest stays invested.

Their HSA balance grows to about $85,000 by the time Marcus retires at 60. When they both need health insurance until Medicare at 65, they use the HSA to cover premiums tax-free‚Äîsaving them from pulling taxable money out of their 403(b)s.
