When Should You Enroll?

The best time to enroll is as soon as possible. Here's why:

  • Time in the market beats timing the market: The earlier you start, the more your money compounds
  • Dollar-cost averaging works in your favor: Automatic contributions smooth out market volatility
  • Tax benefits start immediately: Your contributions reduce your taxable income right away
  • Behavioral momentum: Once you enroll, it's automatic‚Äîyou don't have to think about it

Real talk: Waiting for "the right time" or "when I have more money" is the #1 mistake teachers make. There's no perfect time. The best time was yesterday. The second best time is today.

Typical Enrollment Windows

Most districts allow enrollment during these periods:

  • Open enrollment: Usually in the fall (September-November)
  • New hire enrollment: Within 30-60 days of starting your position
  • Year-round enrollment: Some districts allow enrollment anytime (check with HR)
  • Life event enrollment: Marriage, birth, adoption (usually 30-60 days after event)

Check with your HR/benefits office to confirm your district's enrollment deadlines and windows.

‚ö†Ô∏è Don't Skip the Beneficiary Step!

I cannot stress this enough: You MUST designate beneficiaries. If you die without a beneficiary on file, your retirement account could go through probate, creating legal headaches and delays for your family.

Primary beneficiary: The person(s) who receive your account if you die (usually spouse, children, or parents).

Contingent beneficiary: Backup beneficiaries if your primary beneficiaries are deceased.

Update beneficiaries whenever you have major life changes (marriage, divorce, births, deaths).

Step-by-Step Enrollment Process

Here's exactly what to do. Follow these steps in order:

Confirm Your District's Approved Vendor List

Go to your HR/benefits office or district website and get the official list of approved 403(b) and 457(b) vendors. You can ONLY choose from this list.

Where to find it:

  • District benefits portal (login required)
  • HR office (ask for the "403(b) vendor list" and "457(b) vendor list")
  • Your district's finance/payroll department

Pro tip: If your district doesn't have Fidelity or Vanguard on the list, ask HR if they can add them. Many districts will add vendors upon request.

Get the Enrollment Paperwork

Download or pick up the official enrollment forms from your HR office. You'll likely need:

  • 403(b) Salary Reduction Agreement (SRA): This authorizes payroll deductions
  • 457(b) Enrollment Form: Similar to the 403(b) form but for 457(b) accounts
  • Vendor account application: Some districts require you to open the account with the vendor first, others handle it for you

Ask HR: "Do I need to open the vendor account before submitting the district enrollment form, or does the district set it up for me?"

Open Your Vendor Account (If Required)

If your district requires you to open the account first, go to your chosen vendor's website (e.g., Fidelity.com, Vanguard.com) and complete their online account application.

What you'll need:

  • Social Security number
  • Date of birth
  • Employment information (employer name, start date)
  • Beneficiary information

Time required: 10-15 minutes

Once your account is opened, the vendor will give you an account number. Write this down‚Äîyou'll need it for the district enrollment form.

Complete the District Enrollment Form

Fill out your district's 403(b) and/or 457(b) enrollment form. Here's what you'll typically need to provide:

  • Personal information: Name, SSN, date of birth, address, phone, email
  • Vendor selection: Choose your vendor from the approved list
  • Account number: Your vendor account number (if you opened it in Step 3)
  • Contribution amount: How much per paycheck (we'll cover this next)
  • Contribution type: Pre-tax, Roth, or both (most teachers choose pre-tax)
  • Start date: When you want deductions to begin (usually "as soon as possible")

Designate Your Beneficiaries

This is done either on the district form or directly with your vendor. Don't skip this step!

Primary beneficiaries: List the person(s) who will receive your account if you die. Include:

  • Full legal name
  • Date of birth
  • Social Security number
  • Relationship to you
  • Percentage allocation (if multiple beneficiaries, must total 100%)

Contingent beneficiaries: Backups in case your primary beneficiaries are deceased. Same information as above.

Submit Your Enrollment Form to HR/Payroll

Turn in your completed enrollment form to your district's HR or payroll office. Get confirmation that it was received.

Submission methods:

  • In person (best‚Äîget a stamped copy for your records)
  • Email (ask for a confirmation reply)
  • Online portal (screenshot the confirmation page)

Pro tip: Ask HR, "When will my first contribution be deducted?" This helps you verify everything later.

Verify Your First Paycheck Deduction

After submitting, check your next 1-2 paychecks to confirm the deduction is happening correctly.

What to verify:

  • Deduction amount matches what you requested
  • Deduction is labeled correctly (403(b) or 457(b), not pension)
  • Vendor name is correct

If something's wrong: Contact HR/payroll immediately. Mistakes happen‚Äîcatch them early.

Log Into Your Vendor Account & Verify Deposits

Within 1-2 pay periods after your first deduction, log into your vendor account (Fidelity, Vanguard, etc.) and confirm:

  • Your contributions are showing up
  • The amounts match your paycheck deductions
  • Money is being invested (not just sitting in cash/money market)

If contributions aren't showing up after 2 pay periods: Call your vendor's customer service AND your district payroll office to investigate.

‚úÖ You're Enrolled! Now What?

Once you've completed all 8 steps and verified everything is working, you're officially building wealth. Here's what happens next:

  • Contributions are deducted automatically from every paycheck
  • Money is invested according to your fund selections (see Module 4)
  • You receive quarterly or annual statements from your vendor
  • You can log in anytime to check your balance and make changes

Set it and forget it. Let compounding do the work.

How Much Should You Contribute?

This is the most common question teachers ask. Here's how to decide:

The Quick Answer: Start with 5-10%

If you're brand new to retirement saving, start with 5-10% of your gross salary and increase it by 1% every year (or whenever you get a raise).

Example: If you make $60,000/year, 5% = $3,000/year = $250/month. If you're paid monthly, that's $250/paycheck. If you're paid semi-monthly (24 paychecks), that's $125/paycheck.

The Better Answer: Target 15-20% Total

Remember from Module 5: Your total retirement savings (including pension contributions) should be 15-20% of gross income.

Here's how to calculate your target 403(b)/457(b) contribution:

  1. Find your pension contribution rate: Check your paycheck stub (usually 7-10% of salary)
  2. Calculate 15-20% of your gross salary: This is your total savings target
  3. Subtract your pension contribution: What's left is your 403(b)/457(b) target

üìä Example Calculation

Scenario: Sarah makes $65,000/year and contributes 8% to her pension.

  • Target total savings: 15% of $65,000 = $9,750/year
  • Pension contribution: 8% of $65,000 = $5,200/year
  • 403(b) target: $9,750 - $5,200 = $4,550/year
  • Monthly contribution: $4,550 √∑ 12 = $379/month

Sarah should contribute $379/month (or $189.50 per paycheck if paid semi-monthly).

Percentage vs. Dollar Amount: Which Should You Choose?

When you enroll, you'll choose to contribute either a percentage of salary or a fixed dollar amount. Here's the difference:

Contribution Type How It Works Best For
Percentage Contribution automatically increases when your salary increases (raises, step increases) Teachers who want automatic increases and don't want to manually adjust contributions every year
Dollar Amount Fixed amount per paycheck‚Äîstays the same unless you manually change it Teachers who want precise control over cash flow and prefer predictable deductions
Coach Marty says:
I recommend percentage-based contributions for most teachers. Here's why: When you get a raise, your contribution automatically increases WITHOUT you having to update any forms. This is called "paying yourself first"‚Äîyour retirement savings grows with your income, and you never see the extra money in your paycheck so you're not tempted to spend it.

What If You Can't Afford 15-20% Right Now?

That's okay! Start where you can and increase over time. Here's the strategy:

  • Year 1: Start with 3-5% (just get in the game)
  • Year 2: Increase to 6-8%
  • Year 3: Increase to 9-11%
  • Year 4+: Increase to 12-15% or higher

Every time you get a raise or step increase, increase your contribution by 1-2%. You won't even notice the difference in your paycheck, but it makes a massive difference in your retirement account.

‚ö†Ô∏è Don't Exceed the Annual Limits

Remember the 2026 contribution limits:

  • 403(b) and 457(b): $24,500 per account ($32,500 if 50+)
  • 403(b) AND 457(b) combined: You can contribute up to $24,500 to EACH account in the same year (total of $49,000 if under 50, or $65,000 if 50+)

Your payroll system should prevent over-contributions, but if you're contributing to both accounts, monitor your year-to-date totals to ensure you don't exceed limits.

8 Enrollment Mistakes to Avoid

I've seen these mistakes cost teachers thousands (or tens of thousands) of dollars. Don't let this be you.

‚ùå Mistake #1: Not Designating a Beneficiary

The fix: Fill out the beneficiary form IMMEDIATELY. Don't leave it blank. Update it after major life events (marriage, divorce, births, deaths).

‚ùå Mistake #2: Choosing the Wrong Vendor

The fix: Don't pick the vendor that sends the flashiest brochure or whose rep visits your school. Pick a low-cost index fund provider like Fidelity or Vanguard. Refer to Module 4 for vendor selection guidance.

‚ùå Mistake #3: Leaving Money in Cash/Money Market

The fix: After enrolling, log into your account and verify your contributions are being INVESTED, not sitting in cash. Choose your investment funds (low-cost index funds) immediately.

‚ùå Mistake #4: Contributing Too Little

The fix: Start with at least 5% if you can. Increase by 1% every year. Target 15-20% total savings (including pension). The difference between 3% and 10% contributions is life-changing over 30 years.

‚ùå Mistake #5: Not Verifying the First Paycheck Deduction

The fix: Check your paycheck after enrollment to confirm the deduction is happening and the amount is correct. If it's wrong, contact HR immediately‚Äîdon't wait.

‚ùå Mistake #6: Stopping Contributions During Market Downturns

The fix: NEVER stop contributing when the market crashes. That's when you're buying low. Keep contributing through market volatility‚Äîit's how you build wealth.

‚ùå Mistake #7: Forgetting to Increase Contributions After a Raise

The fix: Set a calendar reminder every August/September (when raises usually happen) to increase your contribution by 1-2%. Or choose percentage-based contributions so it happens automatically.

‚ùå Mistake #8: Not Reading Your Vendor Statements

The fix: Review your quarterly/annual statements. Make sure contributions are showing up, fees are low, and your balance is growing. If something looks wrong, call your vendor immediately.

What Happens After You Enroll?

Once you're enrolled and everything is verified, here's what to expect:

Automatic Paycheck Deductions

Your contributions will be deducted from every paycheck automatically. You'll see the deduction on your pay stub, usually labeled something like:

  • "403(b) Retirement"
  • "457(b) Deferred Comp"
  • "Tax Sheltered Annuity (TSA)"
  • Your vendor name (e.g., "Fidelity 403b")

Your taxable income is reduced by the contribution amount, which means you pay less in federal and state income taxes immediately.

Vendor Statements & Account Access

You'll receive statements from your vendor (Fidelity, Vanguard, etc.) either:

  • Quarterly: Every 3 months
  • Annually: Once per year

You can also log into your account online anytime to check:

  • Current balance
  • Contribution history
  • Investment performance
  • Beneficiary information

Making Changes

You can typically change your contribution amount, investment funds, or beneficiaries anytime by:

  • Contribution changes: Submit a new enrollment form to your district HR/payroll office
  • Investment changes: Log into your vendor account and change your fund allocations
  • Beneficiary changes: Update through your vendor's website or by submitting a beneficiary form

üí° When to Make Changes

Increase contributions when:

  • You get a raise or step increase
  • You pay off debt (redirect that payment to retirement)
  • Your spouse or partner starts working or gets a raise
  • Your kids finish daycare or college

Decrease contributions only if:

  • You face a true financial emergency (medical crisis, job loss)
  • You're redirecting money to pay off high-interest debt (>7%)

Never decrease because: "The market is down" or "I want to buy something." Stay the course.

Annual Limits Reset

Contribution limits reset every January 1. If you maxed out in 2026, you can start contributing again in 2027 with the new limits.

Your payroll system should automatically track your year-to-date contributions and stop deductions when you hit the limit.

Coach Marty's Final Enrollment Tips

1. Don't overthink it. Enrolling is straightforward. Get the forms, fill them out, submit them. Don't let analysis paralysis keep you from starting.

2. Start small if you have to, but START. Contributing 3% is infinitely better than contributing 0%. You can always increase later.

3. Automate the increase. Set a calendar reminder every September to increase your contribution by 1%. Or choose percentage-based contributions so raises automatically increase your savings.

4. Ignore your balance for 5 years. Seriously. Market volatility will make you panic. Don't look. Just keep contributing. Check back in 5 years and you'll be amazed.

5. Share your plan with your loved ones. Make sure your spouse, partner, or trusted family member knows where your retirement accounts are, who your beneficiaries are, and how to access the accounts if something happens to you.

6. Don't wait for "the right time." There's never a perfect time to start saving. Start now. Adjust later if needed.

7. Celebrate the win. You just made one of the most important financial decisions of your life. You're building wealth. You're securing your future. Be proud.
