Life Happens: Adjusting Your Plan

How to stay on track through life's curveballs without derailing your retirement

The Game Plan Changes. You Don't Quit.

You enrolled. You're contributing. You picked solid low-cost index funds. You're building wealth.

Then life happens.

Medical bills. Car repairs. Job changes. Divorce. Kids. Market crashes. Caring for aging parents. Unexpected windfalls. The house needs a new roof.

Life isn't static. Your financial situation will change‚Äîsometimes gradually, sometimes overnight. The question isn't IF you'll face financial challenges. The question is HOW you'll respond when they happen.

Life will test you‚Äîmedical emergencies, job changes, unexpected expenses. The teachers who retire comfortably aren't the ones who never faced obstacles. They're the ones who stayed in the game and adjusted their strategy without quitting.

Should I Pause or Reduce My Contributions?

This is the #1 question teachers ask when facing financial stress. Here's the honest answer:

Pausing contributions should be your LAST resort, not your first move. Every month you stop contributing is a month of lost compounding you can never get back.

Decision Framework: Should You Reduce Contributions?

‚úÖ YES, Consider Reducing If:

1. You have ZERO emergency fund and face immediate bills
Example: $2,000 car repair and you have $50 in savings. Temporarily reduce contributions to build a $1,000 starter emergency fund, then resume.
2. You're drowning in high-interest debt (credit cards >15%)
Example: $8,000 credit card debt at 22% APR. Temporarily reduce contributions to aggressively pay off the debt in 12-18 months, then resume at HIGHER rate.
3. You face a TRUE financial emergency you can't solve any other way
Example: Medical emergency not covered by insurance, job loss, family crisis. Reduce to minimum (3-5%) while dealing with emergency, then ramp back up ASAP.
4. You're temporarily in survival mode (laid off, on unpaid leave)
Example: Taking unpaid maternity leave for 3 months. Pause contributions during unpaid period, resume immediately when paychecks restart.

‚ö†Ô∏è NEVER Reduce Contributions For:

  • Vacations: That's stealing from Future You to fund a trip
  • Wants vs. needs: New car, bigger TV, home renovations (unless critical repairs)
  • Market downturns: This is the WORST time to stop‚Äîyou're buying low!
  • "I'll start saving later": Later never comes. Compounding needs TIME.
  • Short-term cash crunches: Tighten the budget elsewhere first
  • Keeping up with neighbors: They're probably broke. You're building wealth.

The 6-Month Rule

Here's a simple test: Can you solve this problem in 6 months or less without touching retirement savings?

  • YES: Don't reduce contributions. Tighten the budget, pick up overtime, sell stuff‚Äîfind another way.
  • NO: If it's a true 12+ month crisis, consider TEMPORARILY reducing to minimum (3-5%) while you solve it.
Coach Marty says:
Too many teachers pause contributions for a small emergency and never restart. Five years later, they've missed out on tens of thousands in contributions and growth. Don't let that be you. If you MUST reduce, set a specific date to resume and stick to it like a game plan. Better yet‚Äîreduce to 3-5% instead of pausing completely. STAY IN THE GAME.

Changing Jobs? Don't Touch That Money!

When you leave your school district‚Äîwhether you're moving to another district, switching careers, or retiring‚Äîyou have important decisions to make about your 403(b) and 457(b) accounts.

Your 4 Rollover Options

Option 1: Leave It Where It Is

How it works: Your money stays invested with your old district's vendor

Pros:

  • No action required‚Äîeasiest option
  • Familiar platform
  • No risk of missing deadlines or making mistakes

Cons:

  • Harder to track multiple old accounts over time
  • May have limited investment options or higher fees
  • Can't make new contributions

Best for: If you have a great low-cost vendor (Fidelity, Vanguard) and don't mind managing multiple accounts

Option 2: Roll Over to New Employer's Plan

How it works: Transfer your old 403(b)/457(b) into your new district's plan

Pros:

  • Consolidates everything in one place
  • Easier to manage and track
  • Can continue contributing to the same account

Cons:

  • New plan might have higher fees or worse investment options
  • Some plans don't accept rollovers
  • May lose access to unique funds from old plan

Best for: If your new district has excellent low-cost options and you want everything consolidated

Option 3: Roll Over to an IRA

How it works: Transfer your 403(b) to a Traditional IRA, or your 457(b) to a Traditional IRA

Pros:

  • Complete investment freedom‚Äîany fund or stock you want
  • Often lower fees than employer plans
  • Full control over vendor selection (Fidelity, Vanguard, Schwab)
  • Consolidates old 403(b)s from multiple districts

Cons:

  • Loses 457(b) penalty-free withdrawal advantage (becomes subject to 10% penalty before 59¬Ω)
  • Can't take loans from IRAs
  • Requires more active management

Best for: If you want maximum investment control and lower fees, and you're rolling over a 403(b) (think twice before rolling a 457(b))

‚ùå Option 4: CASH OUT (Don't Do This!)

How it works: Take the money as a lump sum

"Pros": You get cash immediately (terrible idea)

Cons:

  • Federal income tax + 10% penalty (403(b)) or just federal tax (457(b))
  • Lose decades of compound growth
  • Destroys your retirement security
  • Cannot undo this decision

Example disaster: Cash out $50,000 at age 35. After taxes and penalties, you get ~$32,000. That $50,000 would've grown to $500,000+ by age 65. You just traded half a million dollars for thirty-two thousand.

NEVER cash out. EVER.

How to Do a Rollover (The Right Way)

Follow these steps to avoid taxes and penalties:

  1. Choose your destination: New employer plan or IRA at Fidelity/Vanguard/Schwab
  2. Open the new account (if rolling to IRA)
  3. Request a DIRECT rollover: Tell your old vendor to send the money directly to your new account (NOT to you)
  4. Complete rollover paperwork: Both old and new institutions will have forms
  5. Track the transfer: Usually takes 2-4 weeks
  6. Verify deposit: Log into new account and confirm money arrived
  7. Invest the funds: Don't leave it sitting in cash!

‚ö†Ô∏è Direct vs. Indirect Rollover

DIRECT rollover: Old vendor sends money directly to new account. Clean, simple, no taxes or penalties.

INDIRECT rollover: Vendor sends YOU a check. You have 60 days to deposit it into another retirement account or you face taxes + penalties. They withhold 20% for taxes. MESSY. Risky.

Always choose DIRECT. Never let them send you a check.

üìñ Real Example: Mike's Rollover

Mike taught in California for 8 years, then moved to Texas for a new teaching job. He had $62,000 in his California 403(b) with a high-fee vendor (1.2% annual fee).

What Mike did:

  • Opened a Traditional IRA at Fidelity
  • Requested a direct rollover from his old 403(b) vendor
  • Money transferred in 3 weeks
  • Invested in low-cost index funds (0.04% fee instead of 1.2%)

The impact: By switching from 1.2% fees to 0.04% fees, Mike will save ~$200,000 over 30 years on a $62,000 balance. The rollover saved his retirement.

Stuck With a High-Fee Vendor? Here's How to Switch

Remember Module 3 when you checked your vendor on 403bwise.org and got a red light (high fees)? You might have thought, "Great, I'm stuck with this vendor forever."

WRONG. You're not stuck.

Most teachers don't realize you can switch vendors while staying at your current job. Here's exactly how to do it:

Can I Really Switch Vendors?

In most states and districts, YES. You have the right to choose any vendor on your district's approved list. If you're currently with a high-fee insurance company and want to move to Fidelity or Vanguard, you can do that.

‚úÖ When You Can Switch:

  • Open enrollment period: Usually in fall (September-November)‚Äîsame time as health insurance
  • Anytime (some districts): Many districts allow vendor changes throughout the year‚Äîcheck with HR
  • After a waiting period: Some vendors require you to wait 1-2 years before transferring out (rare, but check your plan)

The Two-Step Process to Switch

Switching vendors involves two separate actions:

Step 1: Change Your Future Contributions

This tells your district's payroll to send your NEW contributions to a different vendor.

  1. Choose your new vendor: Pick a low-cost provider from your district's approved list (Fidelity, Vanguard, TIAA are ideal)
  2. Open an account with the new vendor: Online application takes 10-15 minutes
  3. Complete a new enrollment form: Get the 403(b)/457(b) enrollment form from HR, fill it out with your NEW vendor information
  4. Submit to HR/payroll: Your district will redirect future paycheck deductions to the new vendor

Result: Your NEW contributions go to the low-cost vendor. But your OLD money is still sitting with the high-fee vendor.

Step 2: Transfer Your Existing Balance (Vendor-to-Vendor Transfer)

This is where you move your EXISTING money from the high-fee vendor to your new low-cost vendor.

üìã How to Do a Vendor-to-Vendor Transfer

  1. Confirm your new account is open: You need an active account at your new vendor first
  2. Request a transfer from your NEW vendor: Call or log into your new vendor (Fidelity, Vanguard, etc.) and say "I want to transfer my 403(b) from [old vendor] to my account here"
  3. Complete transfer paperwork: New vendor will send you forms (or handle it online)
  4. Provide old account information: Account number, balance, old vendor name
  5. Choose transfer type: ALWAYS choose "direct transfer" (old vendor sends money directly to new vendor‚Äîno taxes or penalties)
  6. Wait 2-4 weeks: Transfers take time‚Äîbe patient
  7. Verify the transfer: Log into both accounts to confirm money left old account and arrived at new account
  8. Invest the funds: Don't leave it sitting in cash at the new vendor!

What Happens to My Old Account?

After the transfer completes:

  • Old account balance = $0: The full balance moved to your new vendor
  • Old account stays open (usually): It just sits empty, which is fine
  • You can close it: Call the old vendor and request account closure if you want

Do I Pay Taxes or Penalties?

NO‚Äîif you do a direct transfer. As long as the money moves directly from old vendor to new vendor (and both are 403(b) accounts or both are 457(b) accounts), there are NO taxes and NO penalties.

‚ö†Ô∏è Important Rules for Vendor Transfers

  • 403(b) to 403(b) ONLY: Don't mix account types (can't transfer 403(b) to 457(b))
  • Direct transfer required: Never let them send YOU a check‚Äîalways direct vendor-to-vendor
  • Check for surrender charges: Some annuity-based vendors charge fees to leave (usually 5-7% if you leave within first 5-10 years). This is RARE for mutual fund vendors.
  • Keep contributing during transfer: Don't stop contributions while waiting for the transfer‚Äîkeep building wealth

What If My New Vendor Isn't on the Approved List?

If your district doesn't have Fidelity or Vanguard on the approved vendor list:

  1. Ask HR to add them: Many districts will add vendors upon request (it costs the district nothing)
  2. Check neighboring districts: Sometimes county-level or state-level 403(b) plans have better vendor options
  3. Pick the best available: If you can't get Fidelity/Vanguard added, choose the lowest-fee option on your current list

üí∞ The Cost of NOT Switching

Scenario: You have $40,000 with a high-fee vendor (1.2% annual fee). You contribute $400/month for 25 more years.

Option 1: Stay with high-fee vendor (1.2% fee) $356,000
Option 2: Switch to low-fee vendor (0.04% fee) $462,000
SWITCHING SAVES YOU: $106,000

That's over $100,000 in your pocket instead of the insurance company's pocket. Switching vendors is one of the highest-impact financial moves you can make.

üìñ Real Example: Sarah's Vendor Switch

Sarah discovered her 403(b) vendor (a high-fee insurance company) had a RED LIGHT on 403bwise.org. She was paying 1.4% in total fees. She had $38,000 in the account.

What Sarah did:

  • During open enrollment, she submitted a new enrollment form selecting Fidelity as her vendor
  • Opened a Fidelity 403(b) account online (15 minutes)
  • Called Fidelity and requested a transfer from her old vendor
  • Completed the transfer paperwork (5 pages, took 20 minutes)
  • 3 weeks later, her full $38,000 balance appeared in her Fidelity account
  • Invested in a target-date index fund (0.12% fee instead of 1.4%)

The impact: Over the next 28 years until retirement, Sarah will save approximately $180,000 in fees. That's $180,000 that compounds for HER retirement, not the insurance company's profits.

Total time invested: 1 hour. Return per hour: $180,000. Best hourly rate ever.

Coach Marty says:
Switching from a high-fee vendor to a low-cost index fund provider is like getting a massive raise without asking your principal for a penny. That 1%+ fee difference compounds into six figures over a career. Don't let loyalty to a vendor who's overcharging you cost you $100,000+. Check 403bwise.org. If you see red, make the switch. Your future self will thank you.

Major Life Events: What to Update

Certain life events require you to update your retirement accounts. Don't skip these steps‚Äîthey protect you and your loved ones.

Marriage

‚úÖ Action Items:

  • Update beneficiaries: Add your spouse as primary beneficiary (unless you have kids and want to split it)
  • Review contribution strategy: Dual income = higher combined savings potential
  • Coordinate accounts: Make sure you both know where all retirement accounts are
  • Consider spousal IRA: If one spouse doesn't work, you can still contribute to an IRA for them

Divorce

‚ö†Ô∏è Action Items:

  • Update beneficiaries IMMEDIATELY: Remove ex-spouse (unless court order requires otherwise)
  • QDROs (Qualified Domestic Relations Orders): If divorce settlement splits retirement assets, get a QDRO drafted by a lawyer to avoid taxes/penalties
  • Restart contributions if you paused: Divorce is expensive, but don't abandon retirement savings
  • Update contact information: If you moved, update address with vendors

Having Children (Birth or Adoption)

‚úÖ Action Items:

  • Add children as beneficiaries: Primary or contingent, depending on your wishes
  • Name a guardian: Who manages the money if something happens to you before kids are adults?
  • Resist the temptation to pause contributions: Kids are expensive, but Future You needs this money too
  • Consider 529 plans: After maxing emergency fund and retirement, save for college

Death of a Beneficiary

‚ö†Ô∏è Action Items:

  • Update beneficiaries immediately: Promote contingent to primary, add new contingent
  • Review percentages: If splitting between multiple people, adjust allocations
  • Don't leave it blank: Always have primary AND contingent beneficiaries listed

Buying a Home

üí° Smart Strategy:

  • Don't raid retirement for a down payment: That's robbing Future You
  • If you MUST reduce contributions temporarily: Only during the saving-for-down-payment phase (12-24 months max), then resume at higher rate after closing
  • First-time homebuyer exception: You can withdraw up to $10,000 from an IRA penalty-free (once in lifetime)‚Äîbut you still pay taxes and lose growth
  • Better option: Save down payment from current income, keep retirement contributions going

Caring for Aging Parents

The sandwich generation struggle: You're supporting kids AND aging parents. Don't sacrifice your retirement‚Äîyou can't borrow for retirement like kids can borrow for college.

  • Explore other resources first: Medicare, Medicaid, VA benefits, long-term care insurance
  • Set boundaries: Help within your means, but don't destroy your financial future
  • Family meeting: Siblings share the financial burden if possible
  • Keep contributing: Even 3-5% is better than zero

Market Crashes: Your Buying Opportunity

The market WILL crash while you're investing. It's not "if," it's "when." And when it happens, here's what you need to know:

What Happens During a Market Crash

  • Your account balance drops 20-40% (or more)
  • The news is terrifying
  • Your colleagues panic and stop contributing
  • You're tempted to sell everything and "wait for it to recover"

‚ö†Ô∏è The WRONG Move: Panic Selling

When you sell during a crash, you:

  • Lock in your losses permanently
  • Miss the recovery (which often happens fast)
  • Destroy decades of compound growth
  • Guarantee a poor retirement

Historical fact: Every market crash in history has eventually recovered. Every single one. The S&P 500 has always made new highs. Always.

‚úÖ The RIGHT Move: Stay the Course (or Invest MORE)

When the market crashes, you should:

  • Keep contributing: You're now buying shares at a discount
  • Increase contributions if possible: This is the BEST time to invest
  • Ignore your balance: Don't even look at it for 6-12 months
  • Remember your timeline: You're not retiring for 20-30 years‚Äîyou have TIME

Think of it this way: If your favorite grocery store put everything on sale for 40% off, would you stop shopping? No! You'd buy MORE. Same with stocks during a crash.

Historical Market Performance

Market Event Year Market Drop Time to Recover
2008 Financial Crisis 2008-2009 -57% 4 years
Dot-com Crash 2000-2002 -49% 5 years
COVID-19 Crash 2020 -34% 5 months
Black Monday 1987 -22% (in 1 day!) 2 years

Notice the pattern: Every crash recovered. Investors who stayed invested made their money back (and more). Those who panic-sold locked in losses forever.

Coach Marty says:
During the 2008 crash, teachers who kept contributing watched their balances drop 40%. It was brutal. But those who stayed the course? The shares they bought at rock-bottom prices in 2009 are now worth 5-6 times what they paid. That crash made them WEALTHY‚Äîbecause they didn't panic. The market WILL crash again. Don't be the teacher who sells. Be the teacher who stays in the game and buys the discount.

Getting Back on Track After Setbacks

Maybe you had to pause contributions during a crisis. Maybe you cashed out a 403(b) early in your career (ouch). Maybe you didn't start until your 40s.

It's not too late. Here's how to get back in the game:

Step 1: Restart Contributions Immediately

Don't wait for the "perfect time." Don't wait until you have $5,000 saved. Don't wait until debt is paid off.

Start NOW, even if it's just 3%. You can increase it later. The key is to START.

Step 2: Set a Clear Resumption Plan

If you reduced or paused contributions during an emergency, set a specific date to resume:

  • Example 1: "I'll resume 10% contributions on January 1, 2027 (after car loan is paid off in December 2026)"
  • Example 2: "I'll increase from 3% to 8% when I return from unpaid maternity leave in April"
  • Example 3: "I'll contribute an extra 2% every year for the next 5 years to catch up"

Write it down. Set a calendar reminder. COMMIT to the plan.

Step 3: Redirect "Found Money" to Retirement

Anytime you free up cash flow, redirect it to retirement instead of lifestyle inflation:

‚úÖ Found Money Opportunities:

  • Debt paid off: Car loan done? Redirect that $350/month to 403(b)
  • Raise or step increase: Increase contribution by 2-3%
  • Spouse gets a job/raise: Boost contributions with new income
  • Kids done with daycare: $1,500/month freed up? Boom‚Äîretirement
  • Kids done with college: $500/month? Straight to 403(b)
  • Tax refund: One-time contribution to catch up
  • Inheritance or windfall: Max out IRA, increase 403(b) contributions

Step 4: Catch-Up Contributions After Age 50

Once you turn 50, the IRS allows you to contribute EXTRA to retirement accounts:

  • 403(b): $24,500 standard + $8,000 catch-up = $32,500 total
  • 457(b): $24,500 standard + $8,000 catch-up = $32,500 total
  • Both accounts: Up to $65,000/year if you max both out

If you got a late start or had to pause for several years, catch-up contributions are your second chance.

üìñ Real Example: Lisa's Comeback

Lisa started teaching at 25 but didn't enroll in her 403(b) until age 42 (17 years lost). She kicked herself for waiting so long.

Her comeback plan:

  • Age 42-50: Contribute 15% ($9,750/year on $65K salary)
  • Age 50-65: Max out with catch-up ($32,500/year)
  • Total invested: $565,500 over 23 years

Result at age 65: ~$1.3 million (at 8% average return)

Lesson: She didn't have 40 years to save, but aggressive contributions in her final 23 years still built a solid retirement. It's NEVER too late.

Step 5: Automate the Increase

Set a recurring calendar reminder every September to increase your contribution by 1-2%. Or switch to percentage-based contributions so raises automatically increase your savings.

The goal: Get to 15-20% total retirement savings (including pension) as fast as possible, then maintain it for life.

üìå Key Takeaways

  • Pausing contributions should be your LAST resort‚Äîevery month costs you compounding growth
  • Use the 6-month rule: If you can solve it in 6 months, DON'T touch retirement savings
  • Never reduce for wants, vacations, or keeping up with neighbors‚Äîonly for TRUE emergencies
  • When changing jobs, NEVER cash out‚Äîroll over to new plan or IRA via DIRECT rollover
  • You can switch vendors while staying at your current job‚Äîescape high fees by transferring to low-cost providers like Fidelity or Vanguard
  • Update beneficiaries after marriage, divorce, births, adoptions, and deaths‚Äîdon't leave accounts unprotected
  • Market crashes are buying opportunities, not selling signals‚Äîkeep contributing or increase if possible
