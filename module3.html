Fees: The Silent Wealth Killer

Why a "small" 1% fee could cost you over $100,000 in retirement

The $150,000 Mistake Most Teachers Make

Let me tell you about two teachers‚ÄîSarah and Mike. Both were 35, both made $70,000/year, and both started contributing $500/month to their 403(b).

The only difference? Sarah chose a vendor with 0.15% fees. Mike chose a vendor with 1.5% fees.

30 years later, when they both retired at 65:

$147,893
That's how much MORE Sarah had than Mike.
Same contributions. Same years. Different fees.

Mike didn't lose that money to bad investments or market crashes. He lost it to fees ‚Äî the silent wealth killer that most teachers never even notice.

Coach Marty says:
High-fee vendors count on teachers not doing the math. They make millions from educators who think "1.5% doesn't sound that bad." But when you run the numbers over 30 years? It's devastating. Understanding fees is the difference between a comfortable retirement and struggling to make ends meet.

The Compound Effect (In Reverse)

You've heard about compound interest ‚Äî how your money grows exponentially over time. Well, fees work the same way, except they compound AGAINST you.

Here's what happens with a 1.5% fee:

Year 0.15% Fee Balance 1.5% Fee Balance Difference
Year 10 $87,058 $82,551 -$4,507
Year 20 $246,143 $221,696 -$24,447
Year 30 $576,096 $428,203 -$147,893

Notice how the gap gets bigger every year? That's because fees don't just take your contributions ‚Äî they take your GROWTH too. Every dollar the fee takes is a dollar that can't grow for you.

The Real Cost: A 1.5% fee doesn't mean you lose 1.5% of your money. Over 30 years, it means you lose about 25% of what you COULD have had. That's a quarter of your retirement gone.

Understanding the Types of Fees

Not all fees are created equal, and vendors often hide fees in confusing terminology. Here's what you need to know:

Fee Type What It Is Watch Out For
Expense Ratio Annual percentage taken from your investments. This is the main fee. Anything over 0.5%
Administrative Fees Account maintenance, recordkeeping, and platform fees. $50+/year is high
Sales Loads Commission charged when buying (front-end) or selling (back-end) investments. AVOID COMPLETELY
Surrender Charges Penalty for withdrawing money or leaving the vendor early (common in annuities). AVOID COMPLETELY
12b-1 Fees Marketing and distribution fees. YOU pay for THEIR advertising! Adds 0.25-1% annually
Coach Marty says:
Sales loads and surrender charges are huge red flags. They're designed to lock you in and profit regardless of how your investments perform. Quality vendors like Vanguard and Fidelity don't charge these fees. If a vendor is pushing products with these fees, it's time to look elsewhere.

How to Find YOUR Current Fees

Already have a 403(b)? Here's exactly how to check if you're paying too much:

üîç Fee Check Steps

1
Log into your 403(b) account
Go to your vendor's website and sign in (or create an account if you haven't).
2
Find "Fund Details" or "Investment Options"
Look for a section that shows what you're invested in. It might be called "Holdings," "Funds," or "Portfolio."
3
Look for "Expense Ratio"
This is your main fee. It should be listed as a percentage (e.g., 0.15%, 1.2%, etc.).
4
Check for administrative fees
Look for account fees, platform fees, or recordkeeping fees. These might be monthly or annual dollar amounts.
5
Add it all up
If your total fees are over 0.5%, you're overpaying. Time to consider switching (we'll cover how in Module 7).

Can't find the fees? That's a red flag. Call your vendor and ask directly: "What is my total expense ratio and are there any additional fees?" If they can't give you a straight answer, that's another reason to switch.

üö® Warning: "Free Retirement Planning"

A common scenario at schools: An insurance salesperson shows up offering "free retirement planning" or "free financial advice."

They seem helpful. They bring refreshments. They set up in the teacher's lounge during lunch. They offer to "help" you with your retirement.

Here's what they don't advertise: They're paid commission to sell you high-fee annuities and insurance products. That "free advice" could cost you $100,000+ over your career.

Red flags to watch for:

  • They came to YOUR school (you didn't seek them out)
  • They push annuities or "guaranteed" products
  • They can't clearly explain fees when you ask
  • They pressure you to sign paperwork today
  • They say things like "This offer expires soon" or "Limited spots available"
  • They mention "tax-free growth" without explaining it's the same as any 403(b)

What to do instead: Politely decline. Tell them you're doing your own research. Then look up your district's vendors on 403bwise and choose a green light option yourself.

Coach Marty says:
These salespeople target schools because they know teachers are busy and trust people who seem helpful. But products with high fees and surrender charges can take years to escape from. If something feels pushy or too good to be true, trust your instincts. You have the tools to make informed decisions on your own.
